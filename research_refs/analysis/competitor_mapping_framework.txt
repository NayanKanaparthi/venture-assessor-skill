COMPETITOR MAPPING FRAMEWORK

Purpose
Map the real competitive landscape, including the status quo.

Always analyze five layers
1. Do nothing
   - the customer tolerates the pain
2. Manual workaround
   - spreadsheets, email, consultants, handoffs, internal admin
3. Adjacent substitute
   - a tool not built for this exact use case but used anyway
4. Direct competitor
   - a startup or vendor explicitly solving this problem
5. Incumbent bundle or platform
   - large software suite, existing provider, internal system, or major platform

For each alternative, capture:
- company / method / tool
- target user or buyer
- job solved
- why customers choose it
- strengths
- weaknesses
- pricing model if known
- switching friction
- gaps or wedge opportunity

Questions to answer
- What is the default solution today?
- Why does the market still accept it?
- What pain remains unsolved?
- Is the market crowded or merely noisy?
- What kind of wedge could realistically get adoption?

White-space tests
A founder may still have room if:
- the current solutions are hated or tolerated rather than loved
- the existing tools create heavy workflow friction
- the buyer and end user are poorly served by incumbents
- the market has changed faster than incumbents have adapted
- there is a narrow buyer or use case incumbents underserve

Crowding tests
Be more skeptical when:
- many funded startups already cluster around the same pitch
- incumbents have already closed obvious gaps
- switching friction is high and pain urgency is low
- the founder's wedge is “better UX” only
- the idea is easy to copy and hard to distribute

Output rule
Never output a generic competitor list. The analysis must show:
- status quo
- switching friction
- wedge opportunity
- why the market is still open, if it is
