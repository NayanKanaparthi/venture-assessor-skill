PAIN POINT TAXONOMY

Purpose
Classify pain rigorously so the analysis stays tied to real user problems.

Core pain types
1. Tangible pain
   - physical or concrete difficulty
   - storage, access, safety, manual burden, spatial issues
2. Economic pain
   - excessive cost
   - hidden fees
   - waste
   - low ROI
   - switching cost
3. Emotional pain
   - fear
   - stress
   - frustration
   - anxiety
   - low trust
   - disappointment
4. Logistical / operational pain
   - too many steps
   - fragmented workflow
   - delayed coordination
   - compatibility issues
   - fulfillment problems
   - information friction

What makes a strong pain point
A good pain point is:
- real
- painful
- repeated
- relevant to the target segment
- connected to current behavior

Ranking dimensions
Score or assess pain using:
- severity
- frequency
- urgency
- cost
- emotional weight
- operational drag
- current workaround weakness

Current-state check
Always ask:
- what do users do today?
- why is that good enough for now?
- why does it still fail them?

Quantifying value
Value = improvement over the current state
Look for:
- time saved
- money saved
- risk reduced
- stress removed
- output increased
- completion rate improved
- trust or confidence increased

Important
Interesting pain is not always economic pain.
Emotional and workflow pain can be highly monetizable if they drive costly current behavior.
