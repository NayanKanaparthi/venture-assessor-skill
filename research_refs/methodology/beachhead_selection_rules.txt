BEACHHEAD SELECTION RULES

Purpose
Select one narrow, winnable first market rather than staying broad.

Primary scoring questions
Score each candidate segment from 1 to 5 on:
1. Funding
   Is the target customer funded well enough to buy?
2. Accessibility
   Can the founder reach and learn from this segment directly?
3. Compelling reason to buy
   Is the pain urgent enough that the segment would act?
4. Whole product feasibility
   Can the founder deliver a usable solution soon enough?
5. Competition
   Are existing alternatives weak enough to leave an opening?
6. Leverage
   If this segment is won, does it create entry into adjacent segments?
7. Team fit
   Is the segment aligned with founder strengths, motivation, and context?

Three coherence tests
A true beachhead should satisfy these conditions:
1. Similar products
   Customers buy similar categories of solutions.
2. Similar sales cycle
   Customers evaluate and buy in similar ways.
3. Word of mouth
   Customers can influence one another through networks, communities, or credibility channels.

Selection rules
- Recommend one primary beachhead whenever enough information exists.
- Avoid “all SMBs,” “all students,” “all enterprises,” and similar broad buckets.
- A smaller segment with urgency and accessibility can beat a larger but diffuse segment.
- If the segment does not talk to itself, WOM is weaker and acquisition cost may be higher.
- Winning a beachhead is about focus, not about picking the biggest possible market.

Output requirements
For the chosen beachhead, always state:
- why it won
- why other candidates lost
- what assumptions could change the choice
- how this beachhead could expand into adjacent markets
